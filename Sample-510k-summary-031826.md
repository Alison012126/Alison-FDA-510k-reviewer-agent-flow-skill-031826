I. Executive Summary
The 510(k) premarket notification for the SIGNA™ Sprint proposes a new whole-body magnetic resonance (MR) scanner intended for diagnostic imaging. The applicant, GE HealthCare, asserts that the SIGNA™ Sprint is substantially equivalent to its predicate device, the SIGNA™ Premier (K193282), and references the SIGNA™ Artist for certain identical subsystems. This review aims to evaluate the applicant's claims regarding identical Indications for Use (IFU), comparable technological characteristics, and sufficient non-clinical and clinical performance data to support a determination of substantial equivalence.

The SIGNA™ Sprint is characterized by a 1.5T superconducting magnet, differentiating it from the 3.0T predicate. While core operating principles remain consistent with established MRI technology, specific modifications have been implemented across subsystems, including the magnet, gradient coil, RF transmit/receive chains, and introduction of a new 1.5T AIR Posterior Array coil. The submission emphasizes that the device incorporates pre-cleared Artificial Intelligence (AI) features, indicating a reliance on previously established safety and effectiveness profiles for these software components.

Based on the summary provided, the applicant has addressed key areas required for a 510(k) submission, including a detailed device description, a clear statement of indications for use, a comprehensive comparison to the predicate, and summaries of non-clinical and clinical performance testing. The review will delve into the specifics of these sections to ascertain if the differences in technological characteristics raise new questions of safety or effectiveness, and if the provided data adequately mitigates any such concerns, thus supporting a finding of substantial equivalence.

II. Device Description: SIGNA™ Sprint
The SIGNA™ Sprint is presented as a state-of-the-art whole-body magnetic resonance scanner, fundamentally designed to leverage the principles of nuclear magnetic resonance for diagnostic imaging. Its core function involves exciting atomic nuclei with radiofrequency (RF) pulses within a strong magnetic field, detecting the subsequent RF signals emitted by these nuclei, and reconstructing images based on the spatial and temporal characteristics of these signals. This process enables the visualization of anatomical structures and physiological functions within the human body.

The system is engineered to deliver high-resolution images, optimized signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), and efficient scan times, critical attributes for modern diagnostic imaging. A key architectural element is its 1.5 Tesla (T) superconducting magnet, featuring a generous 70cm bore size to enhance patient comfort and accommodate a wider range of patient sizes. The use of a superconducting magnet, coupled with integral active shielding and a zero boil-off cryostat, reflects advanced engineering designed for stability, reduced operational costs, and minimized fringe fields.

The SIGNA™ Sprint is capable of imaging across various planes—sagittal, coronal, axial, oblique, and double oblique—providing versatility for diverse diagnostic needs. This is achieved through a combination of sophisticated pulse sequences, advanced imaging techniques, and robust reconstruction algorithms. The system's compliance with NEMA DICOM standards ensures interoperability with other medical imaging systems and facilitates seamless integration into existing healthcare IT infrastructures, promoting efficient image archiving, retrieval, and interpretation.

Key aspects of the system design include:

Magnet System: A 1.5T superconducting magnet, sharing the same core technology as conventional whole-body 1.5T systems, but with active shielding and a zero boil-off cryostat for enhanced operational efficiency and safety. The shift from a 3.0T predicate to a 1.5T system is a significant technological difference that requires careful assessment of its impact on image quality and specific absorption rate (SAR) characteristics.
Gradient Subsystem: Incorporates a gradient coil capable of achieving a peak amplitude of 65 mT/m and a peak slew rate of 200 T/m/s. These specifications are indicative of a powerful gradient system, essential for rapid image acquisition and advanced imaging techniques such as diffusion-weighted imaging and functional MRI. The submission notes modifications to reduce DC resistance and AC impedance, implying improvements in thermal management and potentially reduced cooling requirements, which could translate to operational benefits.
RF Transmit Subsystem: Features an embedded body coil designed to reduce thermal effects and improve intra-bore visibility. The RF transmit architecture is specific to 1.5T operations, a direct consequence of the magnet field strength.
RF Receive Subsystem: Utilizes a 1.5T Digitize-Per-Pin (DPP) receive chain architecture. This architecture is designed for high channel count and improved SNR by digitizing signals close to the coil elements, minimizing signal loss and noise introduction. The adaptation to 1.5T frequency is a necessary technological adjustment.
RF Coils: The system includes a newly designed 1.5T AIR Posterior Array. This is a crucial component for spine and body imaging, and its "AIR" designation typically implies lightweight, flexible coil designs aimed at improving patient comfort and signal conformity. The system also supports a comprehensive suite of 1.5T detachable coils for imaging all anatomies.
Patient Interface: A detachable patient table is included, enhancing workflow flexibility and potentially patient access or transfer.
Software Platform: The core software platform encompasses various Pulse Sequence Designs (PSD) and applications. A notable inclusion is the integration of several pre-cleared Artificial Intelligence (AI) features:
AIRx™ (Cleared in K183231): Likely an AI-powered scan planning or slice prescription tool, designed to improve consistency and reduce operator variability.
AIR™ Recon DL (Cleared in K202238): An AI-based reconstruction algorithm, potentially leveraging deep learning to enhance image quality (e.g., noise reduction, sharpness) or accelerate scan times.
Sonic DL™ (Cleared in K223523): Another AI-driven application, possibly focused on accelerating acquisition sequences or optimizing specific imaging parameters.
The inclusion of pre-cleared AI features simplifies the regulatory review for these specific components, as their safety and effectiveness have been previously established. However, their integration into a new system requires verification that the overall system performance, particularly image quality, remains consistent with diagnostic expectations.

III. Indications for Use (IFU)
The Indications for Use statement for the SIGNA™ Sprint specifies its intended clinical application as a diagnostic imaging device. It is designed to produce a wide array of image types, including axial, sagittal, coronal, oblique images, spectroscopic images, parametric maps, and/or spectra, and dynamic images. The scope of anatomical regions for imaging is broad, encompassing virtually the entire human body, including but not limited to: head, neck, temporomandibular joint (TMJ), spine, breast, heart, abdomen, pelvis, joints, prostate, blood vessels, and musculoskeletal regions. The IFU also acknowledges the potential use of contrast agents, depending on the region of interest.

Crucially, the IFU emphasizes that the images and/or spectra generated by the SIGNA™ Sprint reflect the spatial distribution or molecular environment of nuclei exhibiting magnetic resonance. When interpreted by a trained physician, this information is intended to assist in diagnosis. This statement aligns with the fundamental diagnostic purpose of MRI systems.

Comparison of Indications for Use: The submission explicitly states that "The changes in technology do not impact the indications for use. The indications for use have not been changed, other than to reflect the SIGNA™ Sprint product name." This claim is foundational for a 510(k) pathway. If the IFU were to differ significantly from the predicate, particularly in terms of disease or patient population, it could trigger a requirement for a new Premarket Approval (PMA) application or necessitate more extensive clinical data to establish safety and effectiveness for a broader or novel use.

From a regulatory standpoint, an identical IFU (barring device name changes) is a strong argument for substantial equivalence. It implies that the proposed device serves the same clinical purpose, targets the same patient population, and provides similar diagnostic information as the predicate. This simplifies the review process, as the primary focus then shifts to evaluating whether technological differences raise new safety or effectiveness concerns, and whether performance data adequately addresses these. The applicant's reference to the FDA’s guidance document “The 510(k) Program: Evaluating Substantial Equivalence in Premarket Notifications [510(k)]” (July 28, 2014) is appropriate, as this guidance explicitly outlines the criteria for evaluating IFU similarity.

Given the identical IFU, the regulatory burden shifts from demonstrating the clinical utility of a new indication to proving that the SIGNA™ Sprint, despite its technological variations, can achieve the same diagnostic utility for the same indications as the predicate. This necessitates a robust comparison of technological characteristics and performance data.
