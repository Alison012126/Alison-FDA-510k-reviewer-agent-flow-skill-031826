I. Executive Summary
This preliminary review assesses the 510(k) premarket notification for the SIGNA™ Sprint, a new whole-body magnetic resonance (MR) scanner developed by GE HealthCare. The applicant seeks to demonstrate substantial equivalence to its predicate device, the SIGNA™ Premier (K193282), and references the SIGNA™ Artist for certain identical subsystems. The core objective of this review is to evaluate whether the SIGNA™ Sprint is as safe and effective as the predicate device, despite key technological differences, and whether its proposed Indications for Use (IFU) are identical.

A pivotal aspect of this submission is the change in magnetic field strength: the SIGNA™ Sprint operates at 1.5 Tesla (T), whereas the predicate device, SIGNA™ Premier, operates at 3.0T. This difference necessitates a rigorous assessment of how the change in field strength impacts technological characteristics, image quality, and associated safety considerations, particularly regarding specific absorption rate (SAR), acoustic noise, and fringe fields. While the applicant asserts identical IFU, the burden of proof lies in demonstrating that the 1.5T system can achieve diagnostically equivalent or non-inferior image quality and performance for all claimed indications compared to a 3.0T system, or that any differences do not raise new questions of safety or effectiveness.

The SIGNA™ Sprint incorporates several updated and new subsystems, including a 1.5T superconducting magnet with advanced shielding and cryogen management, a high-performance gradient system with modifications for improved thermal management, a 1.5T specific RF transmit/receive architecture, and a newly designed 1.5T AIR Posterior Array coil. Additionally, the system integrates several pre-cleared Artificial Intelligence (AI) features (AIRx™, AIR™ Recon DL, Sonic DL™), whose prior clearances simplify their individual assessment but require verification of their seamless and effective integration within the new system context.

This report will systematically analyze the device description, IFU statement, comparison to the predicate, and summaries of non-clinical and clinical performance data provided in the submission summary, cross-referencing against the comprehensive FDA review guidance for Magnetic Resonance Diagnostic Devices (MRDDs). Our aim is to identify areas of conformity, potential concerns, and outstanding questions that require further clarification or data to support a final determination of substantial equivalence.

II. Device Description: SIGNA™ Sprint
The SIGNA™ Sprint is presented as a sophisticated whole-body Magnetic Resonance (MR) scanner, designed for general diagnostic imaging applications. It operates on the established principles of nuclear magnetic resonance to generate detailed images of anatomical structures and physiological processes within the human body. By utilizing strong magnetic fields and radiofrequency (RF) pulses, the device excites hydrogen nuclei and detects their emitted signals, which are then processed into comprehensive diagnostic images. The system is engineered to provide high-resolution imaging, optimized signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), and efficient scan times, all crucial for effective clinical diagnosis.

Key components and design characteristics, as highlighted in the submission summary and assessed against FDA review guidance for MRDDs, are detailed below:

Device Type: Classified as a Class II medical device (21 CFR 892.1000) under product code LNH (Nuclear Magnetic Resonance Imaging System), requiring a 510(k) premarket notification.
Magnetic Field Strength and Type: The system employs a 1.5 Tesla (T) superconducting magnet with a wide 70cm bore, designed to enhance patient comfort and accessibility. It integrates active shielding to minimize fringe fields and a zero boil-off cryostat for improved operational efficiency and reduced cryogen consumption, aligning with modern MR system design for stability and cost-effectiveness. The 1.5T field strength is a significant departure from the 3.0T predicate and necessitates a thorough evaluation of its impact on image quality, clinical utility, and safety parameters like SAR.
Imaging Planes: The SIGNA™ Sprint supports imaging across sagittal, coronal, axial, oblique, and double oblique planes, offering comprehensive diagnostic flexibility, consistent with standard MRDD capabilities.
Gradient Subsystem: Features a powerful gradient coil capable of reaching a peak amplitude of 65 mT/m and a peak slew rate of 200 T/m/s. These specifications are indicative of a high-performance gradient system, essential for advanced imaging sequences (e.g., diffusion-weighted imaging) and rapid image acquisition. The submission notes modifications to reduce DC resistance and AC impedance, which typically aim to improve thermal management and reduce power consumption, contributing to system reliability and potentially mitigating acoustic noise and peripheral nerve stimulation (PNS) effects. Review will focus on ensuring the described PNS control mechanisms (dB/dt limits, user notification, operating modes) are robust and compliant with IEC 60601-2-33.
RF Transmit Subsystem: The system incorporates a 1.5T specific embedded body coil designed to reduce thermal effects and enhance intra-bore visibility. The RF transmit architecture is optimized for 1.5T operations, a direct consequence of the magnet's field strength. This requires verification of SAR distribution and control within patient tissues.
RF Receive Subsystem: Employs a 1.5T Digitize-Per-Pin (DPP) receive chain architecture. This high-channel count design aims to improve SNR by digitizing signals closer to the coil elements, thereby minimizing signal degradation and noise. Its adaptation to 1.5T frequency is a necessary technological adjustment requiring performance validation.
RF Coils: A newly designed 1.5T AIR Posterior Array coil is included, alongside a comprehensive suite of other 1.5T detachable coils for various anatomies. The "AIR" designation typically implies lightweight, flexible designs focused on enhanced patient comfort and optimized signal conformity. New coil designs necessitate thorough performance (SNR, uniformity) and safety evaluations (heating, decoupling from transmit field).
Patient Interface: The system features a detachable patient table, which can streamline workflow, improve patient access, and facilitate patient transfer. The maximum supported weight and positioning accuracy of this table are critical safety and functional parameters.
Software Platform: The core software platform includes various Pulse Sequence Designs (PSD) and applications. A key highlight is the integration of several pre-cleared Artificial Intelligence (AI) features:
AIRx™ (Cleared in K183231): Likely an AI-powered automated scan planning and slice prescription tool, designed to improve consistency, reduce operator variability, and enhance workflow efficiency.
AIR™ Recon DL (Cleared in K202238): An AI-based deep learning reconstruction algorithm, aimed at improving image quality (e.g., noise reduction, sharpness, artifact suppression) and/or accelerating scan times.
Sonic DL™ (Cleared in K223523): Another AI-driven application, potentially focused on further accelerating acquisition sequences or optimizing specific imaging parameters for enhanced speed and image quality. The prior clearance of these AI features simplifies their individual regulatory review. However, the overall system's performance, particularly image quality, must be verified to ensure it remains consistent with diagnostic expectations post-integration. The submission must also address the general software documentation requirements (e.g., "Basic Documentation Level" as per FDA guidance) and robust cybersecurity documentation given the system's connectivity and AI components, which classify it as a "cyber device" under section 524B(c) of the FD&C Act.
Ancillary Features: The system includes respiratory peripheral and cardiac gating capabilities with wireless connections, enhancing image quality by compensating for physiological motion. These accessories are stated to be identical to those on the predicate/reference devices, which simplifies their assessment.
Standards Compliance: The system conforms to NEMA DICOM standards, ensuring interoperability and seamless integration into existing healthcare IT environments for image archiving, retrieval, and interpretation. Adherence to other recognized consensus standards, particularly IEC 60601-2-33 (4th edition) for basic safety and essential performance, and ISO 14971 for risk management, is paramount. The updated 0.9 mT main magnetic field hazard area definition in IEC 60601-2-33 (4th edition) must be explicitly addressed.

III. Indications for Use (IFU)
The Indications for Use statement for the SIGNA™ Sprint specifies its intended clinical application as a diagnostic imaging device designed to produce a comprehensive range of image types, including axial, sagittal, coronal, oblique images, spectroscopic images, parametric maps, dynamic images, and/or spectra. The device's utility spans virtually all anatomical regions of the human body, specifically mentioning the head, neck, temporomandibular joint (TMJ), spine, breast, heart, abdomen, pelvis, joints, prostate, blood vessels, and musculoskeletal regions. The IFU also acknowledges the potential for use with contrast agents, depending on the specific region of interest and clinical need.

The core purpose, as stated, is to generate images and/or spectra that reflect the spatial distribution or molecular environment of nuclei exhibiting magnetic resonance. This information, when interpreted by a trained physician, is intended to be used as an aid in diagnosis.

Reviewer Checklist for IFU Assessment:

Clarity and Specificity: The IFU statement is clear and specific, broadly defining the diagnostic purpose without making vague or overly generalized claims. It clearly outlines the types of outputs (images, spectra, parametric maps) and the anatomical coverage.
Consistency: The submission summary asserts that the IFU for the SIGNA™ Sprint is identical to that of the predicate device, SIGNA™ Premier (K193282). This consistency is critical for a 510(k) submission. Any discrepancies would require significant justification and potentially additional clinical data. The review will proceed with the assumption that the IFU statements are indeed word-for-word identical.
Scope of Use: The IFU describes general diagnostic use for both imaging and spectroscopy, encompassing the broad scope typically expected of a whole-body MRDD. It reflects the spatial distribution and/or frequency/distribution of nuclei, aligning with the definition in 21 CFR 892.1000(a).
Clinical Claims: The IFU as described does not appear to make specific clinical claims related to disease identification, prognosis, staging, or prevention of morbidity/mortality beyond general diagnostic aid. This is appropriate for a typical 510(k) pathway, which primarily seeks to demonstrate substantial equivalence for a device with established clinical utility.
Protocols vs. Indications: The IFU maintains a clear distinction between general indications and specific imaging protocols. The mention of various anatomical regions implies a suite of protocols, but these are not elevated to specific clinical indications requiring separate evidentiary support.
RF Coils and Accessories: The IFU does not explicitly detail coil compatibility, but the device description confirms the availability of a comprehensive suite of 1.5T detachable coils and a newly designed 1.5T AIR Posterior Array. The compatibility of these coils with the SIGNA™ Sprint system (specifically 1.5T field strength) is implicitly covered.
Based on the provided summary, the IFU appears appropriate for a 510(k) submission, provided its identical nature to the predicate is fully substantiated within the submission's labeling and other documentation. The identical IFU is a critical anchor for the substantial equivalence claim, especially given the technological differences.

VI. Discussion of Substantial Equivalence
The SIGNA™ Sprint's claim of substantial equivalence to the SIGNA™ Premier (K193282) hinges on demonstrating that the device is as safe and effective as the predicate, despite key technological differences. The primary and most impactful difference is the shift in main magnetic field strength from 3.0T (predicate) to 1.5T (proposed device). Other differences include updated gradient coil designs, 1.5T-specific RF transmit/receive architectures, a newly designed 1.5T AIR Posterior Array coil, and the integration of multiple pre-cleared AI features.

Identical Indications for Use: The applicant states that the IFU for SIGNA™ Sprint is identical to that of the SIGNA™ Premier. This is a crucial element for a 510(k) submission. However, the identical IFU for a 1.5T system to a 3.0T system places a significant burden on the applicant to provide robust performance data that substantiates equivalent diagnostic performance across the entire spectrum of claimed anatomies and imaging modalities. While 1.5T MRI systems are widely used and diagnostically proven, they inherently have a lower signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) compared to 3.0T systems, which can impact spatial resolution and scan time. The applicant must demonstrate that any such differences do not compromise diagnostic confidence or necessitate new interpretation criteria.

Technological Characteristics and Safety:

Magnetic Field Strength (1.5T vs. 3.0T): This difference fundamentally alters the MR physics and interactions. While 1.5T is a well-established and safe field strength, the submission must provide comprehensive fringe field maps (including the 0.9 mT contour as per IEC 60601-2-33 4th Ed.), and demonstrate the magnet's temporal stability and spatial homogeneity are clinically acceptable for diagnostic imaging.
RF System and SAR: The RF transmit/receive components are specifically designed for 1.5T operation. RF power deposition (SAR) scales non-linearly with field strength and frequency. Therefore, SAR calculations and in vivo measurements for the SIGNA™ Sprint are crucial to ensure compliance with IEC 60601-2-33 limits (whole-body, partial body, head, local 10g averaged SAR) and to demonstrate that the SAR management system accurately reports and controls energy deposition. The modifications to the embedded body coil for thermal reduction are positive but require verification.
Gradient System: The gradient specifications (65 mT/m, 200 T/m/s) are high-performance. The claimed modifications to reduce DC resistance and AC impedance are expected to improve thermal management. However, comprehensive testing for acoustic noise and peripheral nerve stimulation (PNS) is required to ensure these characteristics are within safe and acceptable levels, particularly considering operating modes (Normal, First Level Controlled, Second Level Controlled) and dB/dt limits.
RF Coils: The newly designed 1.5T AIR Posterior Array and other 1.5T coils require rigorous testing for SNR, uniformity, heating, and proper decoupling from the transmit field. This data must demonstrate that the coils provide diagnostically sufficient image quality for their intended anatomical regions at 1.5T.
Software and AI Features: The integration of pre-cleared AI features (AIRx™, AIR™ Recon DL, Sonic DL™) represents an enhancement. While their individual safety and effectiveness were previously established, their combined performance and impact on the overall system's diagnostic capabilities at 1.5T must be validated. The submission must detail the software development lifecycle (IEC 62304) and provide comprehensive cybersecurity documentation as a "cyber device," including a Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) and a vulnerability management plan.
Performance Data and Effectiveness: The non-clinical performance data (Table 3) is expected to show that the SIGNA™ Sprint can produce images with adequate signal-to-noise ratio, spatial resolution, contrast, and uniformity for all claimed indications, comparable to images obtained from other legally marketed 1.5T MRDDs, and demonstrably diagnostically equivalent or non-inferior to those from the 3.0T predicate. Given the SNR difference, special attention must be paid to how the 1.5T system maintains image quality, potentially through longer scan times, advanced sequences, or the benefit of the integrated AI reconstruction (AIR™ Recon DL).

Clinical Data Rationale: The submission summary mentions "summaries of non-clinical and clinical performance testing." If the IFU is truly identical and the non-clinical data robustly demonstrates that the technological differences (primarily field strength) do not raise new questions of safety or effectiveness regarding diagnostic performance, then extensive de novo clinical testing for new claims would typically not be required for a 510(k). Any clinical data provided would likely be confirmatory, demonstrating that the image quality is clinically acceptable for interpretation by trained physicians or that the new AI features are seamlessly integrated and perform as expected in a clinical setting. The absence of specific clinical claims beyond general diagnostic use supports this approach. However, if the non-clinical data fails to bridge the gap in diagnostic performance (e.g., if specific imaging tasks are significantly degraded at 1.5T compared to 3.0T), then further clinical evidence demonstrating equivalent or non-inferior diagnostic accuracy may be requested.

In conclusion, while the identical IFU provides a strong starting point for a substantial equivalence claim, the significant change in magnetic field strength from 3.0T to 1.5T demands rigorous evidence across all safety and performance parameters. The applicant must clearly articulate how the SIGNA™ Sprint, despite its 1.5T field, achieves comparable diagnostic quality and maintains safety relative to the 3.0T SIGNA™ Premier, particularly highlighting the role of new hardware and AI features in achieving this equivalence.
